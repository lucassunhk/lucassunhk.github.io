The Science of Fish (Part 1)

Posted by Lucas Sun

Fish are animals, like us, so they need to eat. Various fish eat a variety of food, including plankton, tiny fish, crustaceans, parasites, insects, medium sized fish, and many more.

To digest food, fish also have a digestive system. They have about the same system as people. Because fish constantly need oxygen too, they developed lungs and gills. Fish keep their mouths open to allow water to pass though their mouths.

The gills do not need to move. The water just goes though the mouth and the water is either filtered by the gills for oxygen and released or filtered by gills and absorbed by the fish.

Fish also need to excrete the waste piled up in the digestive tracts. They eject though the anus (located near the stomach). Fish, like us, also have a urinary bladder. It works similarly to the anus.

To swim around, fish also have a set of fins. Those fins only help the fish speed up a bit and control direction. It is the tail that helps fish swim. It is like a muscle that pushes the fish forward. Our legs are not evolved from a tail, though.

To have offsprings, fish need a reproductive system. The male have sperms while the female get eggs. Some fish bear the babies like the humans, which means having a baby growing in the mother fish and getting “born”. Most fish have eggs instead.

Some fish change gender after they grow to a certain size or age. These fish go through the change naturally, not by DNA. One example is a species called Wrasse.

Episode 6

The Science of Fish (Part 2)

Posted by Lucas Sun

Fish possess different organs to detect predators and prey. Eyes are the most known, although some fish still do not have eyes. The eyes work similarly to terrestrial vertebrates but are rounder in shape. Some fish can even detect polarized or UV light.

Although it may not seem like it, most fish have nostrils like humans. The nostrils work by detecting chemicals in the water, which is like human’s nose, which detects chemical in the air.

Some fish have a special organ – the electric organ. This organ is a biochemical “battery”. It can be used for a variety of ways. With weak voltage, it can detect prey and predators, whereas with strong voltages, it can stun or kill prey and scare predators.

Those fish which are hunted by the fish with electrical organs need to know when such a predator is nearby, so that they can evolve organs to detect those electrical pulses.

To detect food and predators effectively, fish have evolved a lateral line, which is a line with sense organs and cells located on it. It is usually recognizable from a break in colour.

A fish needs to control these organs. To do that, it requires a brain. The brain of fish is small in ratio compared to other similarly sized birds or mammals, but it makes sense because they are less evolved in comparison.

Evolution has allowed some fish to evolve with a larger brain and even to human size. The Coelacanth or other lobe finned fish probably evolved to land animals.

The Giant Catch

Posted by Lucas Sun

After catching the saw fish, Mr. Fish decided to retire from adventurous fishing, but continued normal fishing. Today was a lucky day of fishing.

Steering his boat to the big blue sea, Mr. Fish felt a cool breeze blowing. “A nice day for fishing,” he muttered to himself. He dropped the hook with the bait attached into the water. He saw a giant shadow moving slowly and silently in the water towards the bait.

Mr. Fish knew this fish was slow-to-bite. He waited as patient as a shark, ready to grab its prey. He discovered a triangular fin moving by, signalling a shark. Suddenly, a wave that shook the boat violently came – it was a big bite.

Mr. Fish immediately reeled the fish in. It was too strong. Snap! The was broken. Red with anger, he tried again with his best rod: Strong Liner 5000. Using the same bait, he quickly got the bite again. The fishing rod, being the best automatic model, pulled the line automatically.

Even with an advanced fishing rod, Mr. Fish still could not reel the fish in easily. Two hours had passed, but neither side has won. Then, the fish gave a wrong turn. Mr. Fish now had the best position. With a lot of effort, he reeled the fish in.

Mr. Fish used a fishing robot to pull the fish out of the water. “No, it couldn’t be. It is a fishing legend!” he exclaimed, for now he caught a whale shark, which was the largest fish species in the world. Mr. Fish almost fainted.

Mr. Fish bought the gigantic fish to his friend working at the museum. He further proved that it was indeed a whale shark. Mr. Fish donated the fish to the museum, as usual. All museum staff were excited.

Mr. Fish saw a large shadow in the water nearby the hook, and he thought it looked like a ray. It had a rhombus shape and a thin tail whipping the water around it. He felt a bite. He tried to reel the fish in, but the fish somehow cut the line and escaped back into the reef.
